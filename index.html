About Congo

Imagine the tiny parts inside our phones and computers, or the batteries powering electric cars. They contain something called cobalt, a crucial ingredient that's often mined in Congo. However, beneath the veneer of innovation and progress lies a sobering reality: much of this cobalt originates from the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC), where its extraction is entwined with narratives of adversity and deprivation.

History of Congo

So, How did cobalt mining begin in Congo? To start, between 1998 and 2006, the Zimbabwean military helped Congo's President Kabila by giving him control over copper-cobalt areas. They made deals where Zimbabwe provided security, investors gave money, and Kabila allowed access to minerals. Western companies were cautious about investing because they worried about the government taking over their assets. On the other hand, China needed cobalt for its batteries, so it started investing in Congo. Kabila also had close ties with China, and when Western banks didn't give Congo money for rebuilding, they turned to China for help. This led to a big deal in 2007 where China loaned Congo money for infrastructure in exchange for mineral rights, changing how cobalt mining worked in the region. However, the cobalt mining industry is associated with serious violations of human rights, such as unsafe working conditions for miners and the exploitation of Congolese labor, including children and forced workers.

Current Congo

Despite the abundance of valuable minerals like gold, cobalt, and copper in its soil, the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) remains one of the poorest countries in the world. The paradox of wealth juxtaposed with poverty underscores systemic issues such as corruption, mismanagement of resources, conflict, forced relocation, and environmental degradation, which have hindered the country's development and prevented its people from reaping the benefits of their natural resources. The mining of cobalt in the DRC is not a choice but a necessity for many. It's a grim reality where families rely on this hazardous work for their daily sustenance, trapped in a relentless cycle of hardship. Breaking free from this cycle seems insurmountable, as it represents the only lifeline available to them amidst a landscape marred by exploitation and despair.
